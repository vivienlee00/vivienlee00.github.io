Infinity Mirror.

Using a shadow box frame, I covered the back panel and front glass pane with one-way mirrored film. This causes the light emitted from the LED strip inside to reflect between the two panels, but still allows some light to pass through the glass panel, giving the illusion of an infinitely long box with lights. In reality, the box has a depth of only about an inch.

Arduino Dice Roller.

Created with an Arduino Uno, I built an LED dice-roller. The LED lights and button are attached to a breadboard, which is then wired to the Arduino, acting as both a power source, and as the CPU. The LEDs are arranged in the shape of a die, and the corresponding lights flash when a random number from 1-6 is "rolled", by pressing the button.

Internet of Things.

By connecting wifi-enabled LED bulbs and lightstrips to an Amazon Echo device, I have completely automated the lighting in my bedroom. A simple call to "Alexa", Amazon's AI voice-assistant, allows me to turn on, as well as change the color/brightness of, the lights in my room. This comes in handy during those days when I am just too lazy to crawl out of bed to turn off my lights. Additional "Internet of Things"/"Smart Home" devices that I have implemented include wifi-controlled power sockets, and automated vacuuming robots.

Trump Tweets.

Trump Tweets is a Processing program that utilizes Markov chains and the Twitter API to generate random tweets that exhibit styles/mannerisms similar to those of President Donald Trump's. To run this program, press the "play" button in the Processing window, and press "New" to generate a new tweet. Pressing "Tweet" will publish the tweet to a fake Twitter account found at https://twitter.com/maga_markov.

World of GUI.

Based off of the indie game World of Goo, this Processing program is named World of GUI. The main objective of World of GUI is to get a requisite number of goo balls to a pipe designed to represent the exit. In order to do so, the player must use the goo balls to construct bridges, towers, and other structures to overcome gravity and various terrain difficulties such as chasms, hills, spikes, windmills, or cliffs. This program features physics/mechanics that mimic those of the original game (and real life). To play, hover your mouse over 2 goos that you want to connect. Press 'G' key to add the goo to your structure (you may need to click on the Processing window when putting down your first goo). If you have hovered over a goo but no longer want to to connect it, move your mouse away from it. Have fun building!
